# GTM Manager (Enterprise Security) — Simulation Assessment

## Company Background

**Vantage Shield** is a Series C cybersecurity startup ($68M raised, most recent round led by Accel in Q3 2025) that builds a cloud-native **Security Posture Management** platform. The product continuously scans enterprise cloud environments (AWS, Azure, GCP), identifies misconfigurations, maps them to compliance frameworks (SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA, FedRAMP), and auto-remediates critical findings. The company has 290 employees and is headquartered in San Francisco with a growing team in London.

Vantage Shield gained traction selling to mid-market companies (200–2,000 employees) through a product-led growth motion — a free tier feeds a self-serve paid plan, and an SDR team handles upgrades to the Pro tier. Annual deal sizes in this segment average $38K ARR. The company now has 410 paying customers and $31M in ARR.

The board has mandated a push into enterprise (5,000+ employee companies) to hit $55M ARR by end of FY2026. Enterprise deals are expected to average $180K–$350K ARR, involve 6–12 month sales cycles, and require multi-stakeholder buy-in from CISOs, Cloud Architects, Compliance leads, and Procurement. The company recently hired a VP of Enterprise Sales (Dana Moretti, started 6 weeks ago) and is building out the enterprise GTM motion from scratch. Your role reports to Dana.

**Key context:** Vantage Shield competes against CrowdStrike Falcon Cloud Security, Wiz, Orca Security, and Palo Alto Prisma Cloud. The company's differentiator is auto-remediation depth — competitors flag issues, Vantage Shield fixes them — but brand awareness among F500 CISOs is low.

---

## How This Assessment Works

This assessment contains **3 connected scenarios** that simulate your first two weeks as a GTM Manager at Vantage Shield. Each scenario builds on the one before it, and what you decide in earlier scenarios will affect the situations you face later. Difficulty increases progressively.

For each scenario, read the context carefully, then respond with whatever deliverable or decision is requested. Write as if you are actually doing the work — not describing what you would do in theory, but producing the actual output (an email, a plan, an analysis, etc.).

**Expected time:** 20–30 minutes total across all 3 scenarios.

## Scenario 1: Building Your First Enterprise Target Account List

**Difficulty:** Easy
**Estimated Time:** 7–8 minutes

### Context

It's your first Monday. Dana Moretti (your VP of Enterprise Sales) sends you this Slack message at 9:14 AM:

> "Hey — welcome aboard! I need something from you by end of day Wednesday. We're kicking off our first enterprise prospecting sprint next week and I need a target account list. We want to start with 5 accounts that make sense for our first enterprise deals. Think Fortune 1000 companies where we have a realistic shot at getting in the door within 6 months.
>
> Here's what we know:
> - Our sweet spot has been companies going through cloud migrations or multi-cloud expansions — that's when security posture becomes a fire drill
> - We've had the most success in financial services, healthcare, and SaaS/tech verticals
> - We're SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certified but NOT yet FedRAMP authorized (that's in progress, expected Q3)
> - Our two best customer references are MedVault (healthcare, 3,200 employees) and FinLedger (fintech, 4,800 employees)
>
> Don't just give me a list of big logos. I want your reasoning for each pick — why would they buy from us, and what's our angle in? Bonus points if you can identify a likely entry point (the person we'd try to reach first)."

### Your Task

Create a target account list of 5 enterprise companies. For each account, provide:

1. The company name (these can be fictional or real — what matters is your reasoning)
2. A 2–3 sentence rationale explaining why this account is a fit and what your angle of entry would be
3. A suggested first contact (title/role, not a specific person's name)

Organize the list in priority order (best opportunities first). At the top of your list, briefly explain the criteria you used to prioritize — why are your top picks ranked above the others?

### Grading Rubric

#### Criteria

For each criterion below, the AI grader must produce exactly two things:
1. **A score from 0–10** using this scale:
   - **9–10:** Exceptional — exceeds expectations for the role; demonstrates mastery
   - **7–8:** Strong — meets expectations with clear competence; minor gaps only
   - **4–6:** Developing — shows some understanding but significant gaps in execution or depth
   - **0–3:** Insufficient — fails to meet basic expectations for the role
2. **A one-sentence comment that cites specific evidence** — The comment MUST reference a concrete detail from the candidate's response (e.g., a specific account they chose, a phrase they wrote, an action they proposed, or something they omitted). Generic comments like "good analysis" or "could be better" are not acceptable. Example of a good comment: "Candidate included two government accounts despite Vantage Shield lacking FedRAMP authorization, showing a gap in ICP awareness." Example of a bad comment: "Account selection was reasonable overall."

This evidence requirement applies to ALL criteria across ALL scenarios below.

**Account Selection Quality**
- 9–10: All 5 accounts are clearly enterprise-scale, the selection reflects a thoughtful ICP (industry, cloud maturity, compliance needs, timing triggers), and the candidate avoids "trophy logo" picks that don't match Vantage Shield's current capabilities (e.g., no government accounts given the lack of FedRAMP)
- 7–8: Most accounts fit the ICP well, with only 1–2 questionable picks; the candidate demonstrates awareness of Vantage Shield's strengths and certification gaps
- 4–6: Some accounts fit the ICP but several feel random or aspirational without clear rationale; limited awareness of FedRAMP or competitive positioning gaps
- 0–3: The list reads like a generic Fortune 500 roster with no tailoring to Vantage Shield's specific situation, or includes fewer than 5 accounts

**Rationale & Entry Point Strategy**
- 9–10: Each rationale cites a specific trigger or pain point (e.g., recent cloud migration announcement, compliance deadline, breach incident) and the suggested entry point reflects understanding of enterprise security buying dynamics (e.g., targeting a VP of Cloud Security rather than the CEO)
- 7–8: Most rationales include a credible reason to engage and the entry points are generally appropriate, though some are generic (e.g., "the CISO")
- 4–6: Rationales are surface-level ("they're a big company in healthcare") and entry points are vague or inappropriate (e.g., targeting the CEO or CTO instead of security/cloud stakeholders)
- 0–3: Little to no rationale provided, or the reasoning doesn't connect to Vantage Shield's product, differentiators, or market position

**Prioritization Logic**
- 9–10: The ordering reflects a clear framework — e.g., accounts with the most urgent need and fewest barriers are ranked highest — and the candidate explicitly states their prioritization criteria
- 7–8: The ordering generally makes sense (stronger fits ranked higher), though the prioritization logic is implicit rather than stated
- 4–6: The ordering appears somewhat random, with strong-fit accounts mixed among weaker ones
- 0–3: No discernible prioritization; the list appears unordered

## Scenario 2: The Pipeline Review That Reveals a Problem

**Difficulty:** Medium
**Estimated Time:** 8–10 minutes

### Context

It's the end of your first week. The enterprise prospecting sprint launched using the target account list you built in Scenario 1. Dana pulls you and the SDR team lead, **Jordan Park**, into a pipeline review. She shares the following data from the first 4 weeks of the enterprise push:

| Metric | Target | Actual |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Outbound sequences launched | 50 accounts | 47 accounts |
| Response rate | 12% | 4.2% |
| Discovery calls booked | 6 | 2 |
| Discovery → Demo conversion | 50% | 50% (1 of 2) |
| Average deal size (pipeline) | $200K | $145K |
| Avg. days in Stage 1 (Discovery) | 14 days | 31 days |

**Jordan** says: "My SDRs are burning through the account list. The messaging templates we brought over from mid-market are getting us flagged as spam by enterprise email filters. Half the contacts we're reaching out to aren't the right persona — we're hitting IT Directors, not security leaders. And the AEs are telling me they're spending 45 minutes on discovery calls just explaining what cloud security posture management even is. These enterprise prospects don't know the category."

**Dana** adds some context: "We also have one deal with a F500 retailer — **Colton Brands** (8,500 employees, $4.2B revenue) — that's been stuck in legal review for 3 weeks. Their security team loves us but procurement is asking for a SOC 2 bridge letter because our Type II report is from 8 months ago." Then she says to you privately after the meeting: "I need you to diagnose what's going wrong and come back Monday with a concrete plan to fix it. Not a 30-page strategy deck — I want 3–5 specific actions we can execute in the next 2 weeks that will move the needle on response rate and discovery calls booked."

### Your Task

Write a **diagnostic memo** (1–2 pages) addressed to Dana that includes:

1. **Root cause analysis** — What are the 3–5 underlying problems driving these numbers? Don't just restate the symptoms; explain *why* they're happening.
2. **Action plan** — For each root cause, propose a specific, executable action that can be completed within 2 weeks.

### Grading Rubric

#### Criteria

**Diagnostic Depth**
- 9–10: Identifies root causes that go beyond the obvious — e.g., recognizes that the category education problem (Marcus's complaint) is a messaging/targeting issue upstream, not just a sales skill gap; connects the persona targeting problem to the ICP definition inherited from mid-market; links the deal size gap to selling individual products vs. platform deals; each root cause is supported by evidence from the data or team quotes
- 7–8: Identifies 3–4 legitimate root causes and connects them to the data; analysis goes at least one level deeper than surface symptoms
- 4–6: Lists the symptoms as problems (e.g., "response rate is low") without explaining the underlying causes, or identifies only 1–2 root causes
- 0–3: Restates the metrics without any analytical framing, or the diagnosis is generic ("we need better messaging") without specificity

**Action Plan Specificity**
- 9–10: Each action is specific enough to execute (not "improve messaging" but "rewrite the cold email sequence to lead with a compliance angle targeting CISOs and VP-level cloud security leaders"); actions are realistic for a 2-week sprint and resource-aware (doesn't propose hiring 5 people in 2 weeks)
- 7–8: Actions are concrete and mostly executable within the 2-week timeframe; some actions could be more specific
- 4–6: Actions are directionally correct but too vague to execute ("fix the messaging," "target better personas") or unrealistic for a 2-week sprint
- 0–3: No action plan provided, or the proposed actions don't connect to the diagnosed root causes

## Scenario 3: The Friday That Tests Everything

**Difficulty:** Hard
**Estimated Time:** 8–12 minutes

### Context

It's Friday of your second week. Your quick wins from Scenario 2 are starting to show results — the SDR team paused the old templates and you helped rewrite the outreach with compliance-led messaging. Response rates ticked up to 7% in the last 4 days.

Then two things hit before 10 AM:

**Crisis 1: The Colton Deal Implodes**
At 8:30 AM, Leila calls you, panicked. Sandra Mullins (Sr. Cloud Security Engineer at Colton Brands, the F500 retailer from Scenario 2) just forwarded her an internal email — accidentally, it seems — between Colton's CISO, **Eric Tanaka**, and their CTO. The email reads:

> "James — I'm leaning toward Wiz. The Vantage Shield product is technically superior for our use case but I can't justify recommending a 290-person startup to the board for a critical security platform. If they go under or get acquired, we're exposed. Wiz just sent over a reference from Home Depot's CISO and a case study from Nordstrom. We don't have anything comparable in retail. I'd feel different if they had a F500 reference or some kind of risk mitigation on the vendor viability question. Decision is Monday — I'm presenting to the board Tuesday. We're out of time."

Additional context on this deal:
- Sandra's team ran a 2-week POC and strongly prefers Vantage Shield's auto-remediation over Wiz
- Wiz is offering a 3-year contract at $190K/year (30% discount). Your standard price for Colton would be $240K/year, and you can discount up to 15% without VP approval
- Colton's board recently approved a "Zero Trust Architecture" initiative with a 6-month deadline for measurable progress

**Crisis 2: FedRAMP Announcement Pulled Forward**
At 10:00 AM, the VP of Product, **Hannah Liu**, posts in #product-launches:

> "Team — we're pulling the FedRAMP authorization announcement forward to next Wednesday. We got our authorization letter yesterday (!!!) and the CEO wants to beat Orca to the announcement. I need GTM support ASAP: press release review, sales enablement materials, and an updated competitive battlecard by Tuesday EOD. Who's on point?"

Dana is on a flight to London for a board meeting and won't land for 6 hours. She texts you before takeoff: *"You're in charge of both. Triage, prioritize, and execute. Brief me when I land."*

### Your Task

Write a response that covers:

1. **Triage & prioritization** — Which crisis gets your attention first and why? What do you delegate or defer on the other?

2. **Colton Brands rescue plan** — You have until end of day Friday. What specific actions do you take to change Eric's trajectory? Address his three objections: (a) vendor viability risk, (b) no F500 retail reference, (c) no risk mitigation. Be concrete — who do you call, what do you say, what do you offer?

3. **FedRAMP launch handling** — How do you handle Hannah's request given the Colton crisis? What do you delegate, what do you own, and what connections do you draw between the two situations?

4. **Brief to Dana** — Write the actual Slack message (under 150 words) you'd send Dana when she lands, covering both situations and flagging decisions that need her input.

### Grading Rubric

#### Criteria

**Triage & Prioritization Judgment**
- 9–10: Correctly identifies the Colton deal as the #1 priority (highest revenue impact, smallest window — decision moved to Monday) while recognizing the FedRAMP announcement directly supports the deal rescue; explicitly articulates what's being traded off and how to handle both in parallel
- 7–8: Prioritization is reasonable and the candidate explains their logic; recognizes the connection between the two crises
- 4–6: Treats both crises as equally urgent with no clear sequencing, or fails to see the connection between FedRAMP and the Colton deal
- 0–3: No clear prioritization, or the candidate focuses on one crisis and ignores the other

**Colton Deal Rescue Execution**
- 9–10: Proposes specific, executable actions for Friday — e.g., (1) call Sandra to ask her to advocate internally, (2) arrange an emergency reference call with FinLedger's CISO, (3) prepare a "vendor viability" one-pager (funding, investor backing, customer growth, contractual protections like source code escrow or SLA guarantees), (4) tease the imminent FedRAMP announcement as proof of enterprise maturity; addresses the pricing angle (creative structure like a 1-year term vs. Wiz's 3-year lock-in rather than a pure discount war)
- 7–8: Proposes 3–4 concrete actions that address at least 2 of Eric's 3 objections; the plan is realistic for a single Friday
- 4–6: Proposes 1–2 vague actions ("reach out to Eric") or the plan is unrealistic for the timeline
- 0–3: No rescue plan, or the plan concedes the deal

**FedRAMP Launch Handling**
- 9–10: Recognizes that the FedRAMP announcement is both a workstream to manage AND a weapon for the Colton deal; delegates specific components to product marketing (e.g., press release) while keeping personal ownership of the competitive battlecard because it's critical for the deal; proposes using the announcement as leverage with Eric ("We're about to announce FedRAMP — this is the enterprise commitment you're looking for")
- 7–8: Handles delegation reasonably and identifies the connection between FedRAMP and the Colton deal; commits to realistic deliverables
- 4–6: Tries to do all of Hannah's request themselves (unrealistic) or delegates everything without seeing the strategic connection to Colton
- 0–3: Ignores the FedRAMP request or fails to see its connection to the Colton deal

**Communication Quality (Dana Brief)**
- 9–10: The brief is under 150 words, covers both situations with status and actions taken, clearly flags decisions needing Dana's input (e.g., pricing authority beyond 15% for Colton), and gives Dana a clear picture without requiring follow-up questions; tone is confident and structured
- 7–8: The brief covers both situations and is reasonably concise; some decisions needing input are flagged
- 4–6: The brief is too long, misses one situation, or fails to flag decisions needing escalation
- 0–3: No brief provided, or it's so vague that Dana would need multiple follow-ups
